The Importance of Forests

With ever-growing concerns surrounding global climate change, it is important that we pay attention to the ways in which the world’s environments have changed across time.

Forests contribute significantly to global biodiversity and climate health. As seen in the chart to the side, they constitute roughly a third of the world’s total land mass.

Moreover, forests are not distributed evenly across the globe. The following treemap provides an overview of how global forest amount is distributed, both by country and by continent:

An important thing to note is that five countries — the Russian Federation, Brazil, Canada, the United States of America, and China — contain over half of the world’s forests.

Forest Decline

As seen in the previous section, forests constitute a large portion of the world. However, the amount of land area covered by forests has been decreasing as far back as 1990, when data was first collected by the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations.

Since 1990, forests have declined by 177,502 hectares.

To provide some perspective, one hectare can contain between 1000-2500 trees. If we take the midpoint of that range, one hectare is roughly 1750 trees.

Assuming each hectare of forest that the world has lost contains about 1750 trees, that means that since 1990, the world has 310,628,500 less trees than it did in 1990. For further reference, 177,502 hectares is slightly more than the size of the United States, which is 174.45 million hectares!

This declining trend is worrying for the future of our environment. Even as far back as 2012, a paper published in Global perspectives on sustainable forest management found that a reduction in the world’s tropical forests can lead to detrimental outcomes such as biodiversity loss and an increase in the greenhouse gas effect (cite). A decline in forest cover must therefore be understood so that it can be addressed.

To better understand global forest decline, there are a few questions that can be asked, which we will address in the following sections:
Where in the world has forest decline occurred? What causes contribute to forest decline?

Where in the world has forest decline occurred?

First, let’s break down the world by continent. As seen in the following dumbbell visualization, Africa and South America saw the greatest decline in forest amount between 1990 and 2020, with 8,632 hectares and 1,830 hectares respectively. There is a slight loss in North America within the thirty years. These are huge decreases for our planet and we will focus on the following section. On the other hand, there is an increase within Europe, Asia and Oceania, which implies that the restoration of forests and landscape have better achievements.

We can also break down the world further, by country. To better understand where forest amount has grown over time, we can examine the change in forest percentage from 1990 to 2020. In the following visualization, the change in forest percentage for each country in the world, colored by continent, can be seen:

Let’s reorganize. If we look at the percentages grouped by continent, it can be seen that Europe has seen an overall positive change in forest amount from 1990 to 2020, indicating that its forest amount is greater than it was in 1990. Given this information, it may be worth looking into conservation and reforestation efforts undertaken across Europe as they seem to have been successful at warding off forest decline over the past 30 years. One initiative, known as SUPERB, in Europe recently aims to plant 20,000 hectares of trees in France (cite).

On the other hand, and as we have seen before, Africa and South America seem to have significant overall negative changes in forest coverage. 10 Countries with the Greatest Forest Cover

The chart to the right showcases the ten countries with the greatest amount of forest coverage in the world and the change in the percentage of forests in the country from 1990-2020. On the whole, it can be seen that these countries have mostly seen an increase in forest percentage. However, the United States, Russia, and China have all seen steady decreases since 1990. These are heavily industrialized nations as opposed to the rest of the countries depicted.

This finding provides evidence for the claim that there is potentially a relationship between human activity and forest decline.

What causes contribute to forest decline?

In addition to where forest decline has occurred, we explore some potential causes of global forest decline. The following sections of this article will explore potential causes, both natural and human, of forest decline to better understand how forest decline might have occurred over the past 30 years.

Wildfire

This chart shows how the wildfire affects forest cover rate in countries with the most forest from 1990 to 2020. The width of the line represents the forest area of a country. The red color indicates more fires happened in the last 30 years, while yellow indicates there are less. The vertical position represents the forest cover rate, from 0.1 to 0.8. The left and right side correspond to the forest cover rate in 1990 and 2020.

Countries suffering from more wildfires (the red lines) are mostly experiencing a drop in forest cover rate, like Brazil, Australia and argentina. Countries with less wilde fires (the yellow lines) are mostly experiencing an increase in forest cover rate, like the Russian Federation, USA and China.

Drought

Retrieving the public drought dataset from the U.S. Drought Monitor, we can examine whether there is a relationship among deforestation and drought in the United States.

From the line chart above, we can notice that the growth of forests in the States experienced its highest surge in 2010, followed by a decline in 2017. Compared with the patterns of drought over the corresponding timeframes, it was not in a drought situation during these two years. There’s no substantial correlation emerging among the potential factors contributing to the decline of the forest.
